Toronto Homicide Patterns Analysis

Comprehensive EDA and machine learning analysis of Toronto Police Service homicide records from 2004–2025, identifying temporal patterns, geographic concentrations, and building classification models to predict incident type.

🔍 Business Analytics — 2026 PythonEDACRISP-DM Random ForestDecision Tree

Key Results

56.21% Random Forest Accuracy
83% Recall (Shooting Classification)
52% Shootings as % of Cases
3 High-Concentration Divisions

Problem Statement

Understanding homicide patterns is critical for informed policing and community safety policy. This project applied the CRISP-DM framework to 21 years of Toronto homicide data, aiming to surface hidden patterns by time, location, and incident type — and to evaluate whether machine learning could reliably classify homicide types for proactive resource deployment.

Methodology (CRISP-DM)

  • Business Understanding: Identify geographic hotspots, seasonal trends, and classify incident types
  • Data Understanding: TPS homicide records 2004–2025; EDA to assess distributions, missing values, and outliers
  • Data Preparation: Encoding categorical variables, handling nulls, class balancing
  • Modeling: Decision Tree and Random Forest classifiers (scikit-learn); 80/20 train-test split
  • Evaluation: Accuracy, precision, recall, F1; compared to 54.25% baseline
  • Deployment: Findings presented as policy-oriented recommendations

Key Findings

  • Shootings account for 52% of all homicide cases — the dominant method over the 21-year period
  • Three police divisions — D31, D51, D42 — account for a disproportionate concentration of incidents
  • Clear July–August seasonal spike identified consistently across years
  • Random Forest achieved 56.21% accuracy, surpassing the 54.25% baseline, with 83% recall for shooting classification
  • High recall for shootings indicates the model is effective at flagging shooting incidents — valuable for resource pre-deployment
