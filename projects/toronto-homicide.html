Project Overview & Problem Statement

This project analyzed spatial, temporal, and categorical patterns of homicide incidents in Toronto (2004-2025) using the Toronto Police Service open dataset. The goal was to move beyond reactive policing by building a data-driven system that supports proactive resource allocation and better emergency dispatch decisions.

Descriptive: Map 21 years of crime patterns: trends, geographic hotspots, and seasonality across Toronto's 158 neighbourhoods.
Predictive: ML models to classify homicide type (Shooting / Stabbing / Other) from location and temporal signals.
Prescriptive: Actionable recommendations for Toronto Police Service resource allocation and dispatch optimization.

Research questions:

  • Which neighbourhoods have the highest and lowest homicide concentrations?
  • What type of homicide occurs most frequently, and how has this changed over time?
  • Is there a specific month or day of the week when incidents occur most frequently?
  • Which year had the highest homicide rate in Toronto?
  • Are incidents geographically clustered, and do clusters correspond to specific police divisions?

Dataset & Preprocessing

Source: Toronto Police Service Homicide Open Data. 1,526 records, 18 features, 21 years - zero missing values and zero duplicates after cleaning.

  • Converted OCC_DATE from string to datetime; validated all coordinates within Toronto bounds
  • Dropped: OBJECTID, EVENT_UNIQUE_ID, HOOD_140, NEIGHBOURHOOD_140, projected x/y coordinates
  • Label-encoded: DIVISION, NEIGHBOURHOOD_158, OCC_MONTH, OCC_DOW
  • Target: HOMICIDE_TYPE (3 classes: Shooting=1, Stabbing=2, Other=0)
  • 80/20 stratified split (random_state=42): 1,220 training / 306 test records

Key Findings & Prescriptive Recommendations

Hyper-Local Resource Allocation

Increase community policing in Divisions D31 (North York/West End), D51 (Downtown East), and D42 (Scarborough/North East). Deploy dedicated units in Mount Olive-Silverstone-Jamestown.

21-year data shows these 3 divisions account for a disproportionate share of all homicides city-wide.

Summer Safety Surge (July-August)

Execute a proactive "Safety Surge" in Q3 each year with increased patrol presence and community engagement, especially on weekend evenings when risk is highest.

Consistent July-August spike across all 21 years - stable, predictable, and plannable in advance.

Firearm-Specific Prevention Programs

Redirect resources toward gun buybacks, stronger enforcement against illegal firearms, and youth diversion efforts rather than generalized anti-violence programs.

Shootings represent ~52% of all cases and are the primary driver of rising trends since 2016.

Deploy Random Forest as Decision-Support Tool

Integrate the model into TPS dispatch workflows to estimate likely homicide type in real time, enabling dispatchers to send the correct specialized unit faster.

56.21% accuracy from location and time data alone meaningfully improves over naive dispatch decisions.
